Overview

In HW4, we implemented a physical cloth simulator based on a mass-spring system.
1. Mass-Spring System: Modeled the cloth as a grid of point masses connected by three types of springs (structural, shearing, and bending) to capture its stretchy and flexible behavior.
2. Numerical Integration: Updated point mass positions every time step using Verlet integration. Applied position constraints that limit spring extension for simulation stability.
3. Collision Handling: Implemented collision response for sphere and plane primitives. Also implemented self-collision using spatial hashing to prevent the cloth from clipping through itself.
4. Shaders: Using GLSL, implemented several shader programs to improve visual realism, including Blinn-Phong, texture mapping, bump/displacement mapping, and mirror reflection.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

About ks
The ks, spring constant, means stiffness of springs.
When ks is low(1), the spring is very weak and stretches easily because it cannot hold the force of gravity. The cloth also sags down deeply after it stops moving.
When ks is high(10000), the springs become very strong and the cloth stays tight both while moving and at rest.

About density
The density defines the mass of the cloth.
When density is low(1), the cloth becomes very light, like a feather. It receives very little gravitational force, so it is not sagged at the resting state.
When density is high(1000), the cloth becomes very heavy and sags deeply under its own weight.

About damping
The damping means energy loss of cloth.
When damping is low(0.0), the cloth retains its energy with very little loss. It swings wildly and behaves chaotically, taking a long time to stop.
When damping is high(1.0), the cloth falls slowly and smoothly. It reaches a rest state quickly without oscillating.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

Sphere Collision Implementation
1. Calculate the distance between the point mass and the sphere's origin.
2. If the distance is less than or equal to the sphere's radius, a collision has occurred.
3. Find exact tangent point on sphere's surface.
4. Compute correction vector and update the position with friction of surface.

Plane Collision Implementation
1. Determine if a point mass has crossed the plane with dot product of plane's normal vector and each last position and current position.
2. If the signs differ, a collision has occurred.
3. Calculate the intersection point of the plane and ray. Also add an extremely small surface offset to prevent self-collision.
4. Apply the correction vector from the last position, scaled by surface friction.

Differences in varying ks
1. ks = 500 : The cloth is very stretchy and flexible. It sags deeply and wraps closely around the sphere.
2. ks = 5000 : Cloth has moderate stiffness. It wraps the sphere naturally, like standard cotton fabric.
3. ks = 50000 : The cloth is very stiff. It resists bending and forms large rigid folds that stick out.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Self-Collision Implementation
To implement self-collision, I used spatial hashing to optimize the collision detection.
1. Divide 3D space into discrete boxes and map a point's position to a unique float hash.
2. Map hash values to a vector of pointers and rebuild this map at every time step.
3. For each point mass, retrieve all candidate points in the same spatial box.
4. If the distance to candidate is less than (2 * thickness), calculate repulsive correction vector.
5. Average the accumulated correction vectors and scale down by the number of simulation steps to apply forces smoothly and prevent the simulation from blowing up.

Differences in varying density
1. density = 1 : The cloth is very light. It falls gently and forms large smooth wrinkles.
2. density = 100 : The cloth is very heavy. It falls fast and forms small, chaotic, rough wrinkles.

Differences in varying ks
1. ks = 1 : Cloth becomes limp and fluid-like. It lacks structural stiffness and crumples into many tiny folds, spreading flat on the plane.
2. ks = 100000 : The cloth becomes very stiff. It resists bending and forms large, broad folds.

Part 5: Shaders

Shader Program
A shader program is an isolated program that runs in parallel on the GPU. It is designed to accelerate specific sections of the graphics pipeline and computes lighting, material effects, and vertex positions.
1. Vertex Shader: It processes every vertex on a 3D model. It applies transformations to compute the final position of each vertex and also outputs attributes such as normals and tangents for use in the fragment shader.
2. Fragment Shader: After the vertex shader runs, rasterization produces fragments. The fragment shader takes the barycentric-interpolated vertex attributes as input and computes the final color of each pixel.

Blinn-Phong

Blinn-Phong shading model is an efficient model to simulate the interaction of light with surface. It produces realistic lighting with sum of three different light components.
1. Ambient : Constant baseline illumination that simulates indirect light bouncing around the environment. Prevent complete black shadow.
2. Diffuse : Matte color of the object. Use Lambert's cosine law, so that it gets brighter the more when it face the light directly.
3. Specular : Bright and shiny highlight that reflects directly into the camera. Computed efficiently by taking the dot product of the surface normal and the half vector between the light and view directions.

Bumping & Displacement

Bump VS Displacement
1. Bump Mapping : Only the normal vectors of the fragments are modified to create the illusion of bumps and wrinkles based on the height map. The actual geometry of the mesh remains untouched, so the outer silhouette of the sphere stays smooth and round.
2. Displacement Mapping : Goes a step further by actually moving the physical positions of mesh vertices along their normals, based on the height map. This creates real geometric detail, so the outer silhouette of the mesh becomes visibly jagged and deformed to match the texture.

Coarseness Comparison : (-o 16 -a 16) VS (-o 128 -a 128)
1. Bump Mapping : Mesh coarseness does not directly change the visual output. Lighting is computed entirely per-pixel in the fragment shader, so the surface still looks detailed even on a low-resolution sphere.
2. Displacement Mapping : Mesh coarseness is critical here. A low-resolution sphere lacks enough vertices to represent the high-frequency details of the height map, resulting in a blocky, spiky, and unnatural shape. A high-resolution sphere has enough vertices to accurately follow the texture detail, producing a realistic and smoothly bumped geometry.
